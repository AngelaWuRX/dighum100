01 — Research Framing

The question
behind the numbers

Research Question

How do patterns of investment spending in the United States differ across income groups, and how might these differences affect long-term quality of life and wealth inequality trends?

Significance

Our project bridges the gap between income inequality's present-day impacts and future prospects through the lens of investment spending, consumer expenditures, and trends. While most analyses of inequality focus on what people earn today, we ask: what are they building for tomorrow?


By examining BLS Consumer Expenditure data alongside Federal Reserve survey data on retirement and stock ownership, we map the structural divergence in how different income classes allocate resources — and how those allocations compound over time into vastly different futures.

Theoretical Framing

Our project views quality-of-life differences through the lens of Data Feminism, as developed by D'Ignazio and Klein. Their central claim — that data is never a neutral distributor of meaning, but always shaped by structural forces — anchors our interpretive approach.


We highlight the hidden narratives within our data and are attentive to both the surface patterns and the deeper implications of what the data indicates and, crucially, what it omits. Race, for instance, is absent from our primary dataset — a limitation we address explicitly rather than paper over.


We also draw on Hepworth and Church's framework for ethical data visualization, applying it to decisions about axis scales, category aggregation, and chart structure.

Description of Dataset

Our primary source is the Bureau of Labor Statistics Consumer Expenditure Survey (CEX) 2024, which tracks how American households allocate spending across categories such as housing, food, transportation, education, healthcare, entertainment, and pensions. The survey is stratified by income bracket, enabling direct comparison of spending share distributions across the income spectrum.

We supplement the CEX with the Federal Reserve's Survey of Consumer Finances (2022), which captures data on stock ownership, retirement plan participation, and asset accumulation — metrics that are not available in the spending-focused CEX but are essential to understanding investment behavior at higher income levels.

Data Cleaning Process
01
The raw BLS Excel file uses multi-row headers and merged title cells. We loaded it with header=None and manually extracted income bracket labels from row 2, renaming them to shorter versions (<$15k, $15–29k, etc.) for chart readability.
02
To extract spending data, we scanned column A for category names and checked whether the next two rows contained "Mean" and "Share" in sequence. Blocks that did not match this structure were skipped entirely.
03
Cells containing placeholder values like c/ (suppressed or confidential data) were dropped rather than imputed. No values were fabricated or estimated.
04
We retained only the 14 top-level expenditure categories to avoid double-counting. Subcategories like "Food at home" or "Shelter" roll up into their major parent categories. For each income group, an "Other" bucket was calculated as 100% minus the sum of selected categories, ensuring stacked bars sum to 100%.
05
The "All consumer units" aggregate column was excluded from charts — it is a population-wide mean and would misrepresent the income-stratified comparison at the core of our analysis.
Transparency note: A small rounding gap of approximately ±0.5% exists in some income groups due to BLS rounding in the source table. All values come directly from published BLS figures — nothing was imputed. We also recognize that collapsing subcategories into top-level ones may mask differences within each income group. We made these choices to keep visualizations legible, not to hide complexity.

Introduction

Income inequality is deeply connected to the structure of the U.S. economy and the uneven distribution of income, assets, and investment opportunities. While the immediate impacts of income inequality can be reflected in different levels of consumer expenditure, our project focuses on the future trends caused by income inequality. Our data from the Bureau of Labor Statistics contains surveys about consumer expenditures, and surveys from the Federal Reserve are focused on investment spending.

This project seeks to answer: how do patterns of investment spending in the United States differ across income groups, and how might these differences affect long-term quality of life and wealth inequality trends? What follows is an analysis of five key spending categories through which those diverging futures become legible.

The gap is not simply that wealthy households spend more. It is that they spend differently — in ways that generate future wealth, while lower-income households spend in ways that are consumed entirely by necessity.

Housing as Asset vs. Expense

Investment spending patterns aren't immediately obvious in the data. The complexity in determining whether a certain item constitutes "investment spending" is perhaps clearest in the category of housing. Housing spending can mean expenditures on rent or on mortgage debt — and the difference between these two is profound. While rent is purely an expense, mortgage payments appreciate in the form of property equity. Furthermore, mortgage interest is deductible under U.S. tax policy, meaning the impacts on both current quality of life and future prospects diverge significantly between these two spending modes.

The trend illustrated in Visualization 2 highlights a declining proportion of housing spending as income rises. This reflects a conceptual shift: housing moves from being a financially dominant necessity to functioning as an investment vehicle. This is additionally supported by Pew Research Center data, which shows that homeowners tend to hold significantly more wealth than renters — a gap that is not explained by income alone but is compounded by differential access to the homeownership pathway in the first place.

The Education Spending Curve

Education is one of the most significant financial investments for many Americans. Many families save for college, and many newly admitted students face the prospect of student debt. The trend for education spending shown in Visualization 2 introduces an inverse bell curve — a pattern that encodes a fundamental truth about income and education access.

Education spending as a share of total expenditures decreases between the lowest and median income brackets, then increases sharply between the median and highest bracket. This indicates that wealthier households prioritize education as a forward investment, while middle-income households face constrained tradeoffs between education and more immediate necessities. The lowest-income households, counterintuitively, show relatively higher education spending shares in part because their total expenditure bases are smaller — a reminder that shares can obscure absolute amounts.

Pensions, Insurance, and Compounding Wealth

Pension and personal insurance spending highlights perhaps the most consequential trend in our data. The increasing proportion that higher-income families spend on pensions and personal insurance underscores the significance of gains compounded over time. Many employers offer match-back policies — additional funding toward employee retirement that effectively functions as a salary supplement invisible in expenditure surveys. The increase in pension spending is amplified further by these policies.

The addition of compounding interest on retirement contributions means the exponential wealth gap between lower and higher income households continues to accelerate. A household contributing 10% of income to a 401(k) with a 3% employer match at age 25 accumulates qualitatively more wealth over 40 years than a household that cannot afford retirement contributions at all — and that gap is structural, not a product of individual choices alone.

Crises as Accelerants of Inequality

Events like the Great Recession and COVID-19 have intensified these trends, as highlighted in the digital humanities project The Income Gap. While financial crises generate widespread anxiety and hardship, wealthy households with substantial capital reserves are positioned to benefit. Investment opportunities become more attractive when asset prices fall broadly. Government policy also amplifies this effect: prime interest rate reductions during crises produce more favorable debt and mortgage rates, and those rates carry their advantages forward into subsequent years.

Lower-income households, without liquidity reserves, are more likely to liquidate assets at depressed prices or to incur high-interest debt during crises — the opposite of the countercyclical investment behavior available to high-income households. The crisis does not pause inequality; it accelerates it.

The Entertainment Plateau

Surprisingly, entertainment expenditure remains relatively constant as a share of total spending across income groups. This constancy encodes something important: at higher income levels, households are not simply redirecting marginal spending to leisure. Instead, the proceeds of investment activity are being reinvested. The pattern suggests that high-income families maintain similar entertainment shares not because they are spending less on entertainment in absolute terms, but because investment activity grows to fill an ever-larger share of their financial portfolio.

This has generational implications. Families that are compounding investment gains rather than consuming them can transmit wealth to their children — enabling those children to potentially live off investment proceeds rather than earned income. This intergenerational wealth transfer mechanism, operating silently within the spending data, is one of the most powerful structural engines of wealth inequality.

Race and Hidden Dimensions

Taking all of the above into consideration alongside racial representation in each income class generates critical insights that our data alone cannot fully supply. Different income classes have disproportionate racial compositions: Black and Latino households are overrepresented in lower income brackets relative to their share of the population, while white and Asian households are overrepresented at the top. The differences in quality of life between income classes therefore encode — and reproduce — even greater differences in quality of life between racial communities.

Our primary dataset does not include race. This is not a neutral omission; as Data Feminism reminds us, what gets left out of data is itself a structural choice. Pairing the CEX with external sources, such as SHRM reporting on racial wage gaps and the Federal Reserve's own demographic breakdowns in the SCF, reveals a pattern of compounded disadvantage: lower income, lower housing equity access, lower pension contribution rates, and lower stock ownership — all concentrated along racial lines. We name this limitation explicitly rather than treating our dataset as a complete picture.

The Income Gap — UCLA HumSpace

Relevance Visibility in Project
This project analyzes how U.S. household wealth differs across demographic groups and how crises such as the Great Recession and COVID-19 have widened these gaps. We use the U.S. Consumer Expenditure Survey to study investment-related behavior, focusing on categories like mortgage payments across income classes, while The Income Gap project complements this by showing longer-term wealth outcomes.

Nine Charts about Wealth Inequality in America — Urban Institute
https://apps.urban.org/features/wealth-inequality-charts/
Relevance Visibility in Project
This interactive web feature uses charts and narrative text to explain how wealth inequality has changed over roughly the last 60 years and how it varies by demographic factors, including race and age. It also explains what is driving inequities and how policy choices can help close wealth gaps, which aligns with our interest in connecting consumer expenditure behavior to broader structural patterns in society.
This is a model for turning complex distribution data into accessible visuals with guided interpretation. The structure of each chart — introduced by a short explanation and followed by a key takeaway — directly informs our visualization captions and the framing we provide before each chart. The project's explicit discussion of drivers and policy implications encouraged us to end our analysis sections with reflections on what spending gaps might mean for inequality, moving beyond pure data description.
The Unequal States of America — Economic Policy Institute
https://www.epi.org/multimedia/unequal-states-of-america/

B — Theoretical Readings
Ethical Data Visualization: Taming Treacherous Data
Hepworth, K. & Church, C. (2018). Digital Humanities Quarterly, 12(4). https://dhq.digitalhumanities.org/vol/12/4/000408/000408.html
Relevance Visibility in Project
Hepworth and Church argue for careful consideration of how visualizations can distort history or current events — often unintentionally, as efforts to highlight one issue can communicate the false impression that it is the only issue at hand. They outline an ethical workflow from data definition and collection through visualization and publication, designed to minimize harm while maintaining explanatory power.
This source is present throughout our entire processing section. When we decided how to aggregate income brackets and which variables to highlight, we explicitly considered whose circumstances might be underrepresented or misrepresented. All design choices — axis scales, baselines, number of income groups — could change how large or small the gaps between income groups appear. Hepworth and Church's framework pushed us to justify each choice rather than defaulting to convenience.
Data Feminism
D'Ignazio, C. & Klein, L. F. (2020). Data Feminism. MIT Press.
Relevance Visibility in Project
D'Ignazio and Klein argue that data science is informed by intersectional feminism and that researchers should pay attention to what gets left out and how structural forces like racism and sexism shape those omissions. This matters for our project because the Consumer Expenditure Data and Survey of Consumer Finances do not merely reflect expenditure behavior — they organize financial life into categories that already make some experiences visible and others harder to see.
The Data Feminism framework shapes our entire interpretive stance. It shows up in the way we define and clean our data, in our transparency about missing and limited data (particularly around race), and in the way we discuss the hidden narratives within spending patterns. On the final website, the framework is visible in our explicit methodological transparency section and in the analysis's discussion of race — a dimension absent from our primary dataset that we nonetheless name and address rather than ignore.
